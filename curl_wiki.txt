					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1236090951">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Bunker oil" redirects here. For the Norwegian company, see <a href="/wiki/Bunker_Oil_(company)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bunker Oil (company)">Bunker Oil (company)</a>.</div>
<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Fuel oils of a tar-like consistency</div>
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Residual_fuel_oil.JPG" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Residual_fuel_oil.JPG/269px-Residual_fuel_oil.JPG" decoding="async" width="269" height="400" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Residual_fuel_oil.JPG/403px-Residual_fuel_oil.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Residual_fuel_oil.JPG/538px-Residual_fuel_oil.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1865" data-file-height="2773" /></a><figcaption>Tar-like consistency of heavy fuel oil</figcaption></figure>
<p><b>Heavy fuel oil (HFO)</b> is a category of <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">fuel oils</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Tar" title="Tar">tar</a>-like consistency. Also known as <b>bunker fuel</b>, or <b>residual fuel oil</b>, HFO is the result or remnant from the distillation and <a href="/wiki/Cracking_(chemistry)" title="Cracking (chemistry)">cracking</a> process of <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>. For this reason, HFO is contaminated with several different compounds including <a href="/wiki/Aromatics" class="mw-redirect" title="Aromatics">aromatics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_pollution" class="mw-redirect" title="Nitrogen pollution">nitrogen</a>, making emissions upon combustion more polluting compared to other fuel oils.<sup id="cite_ref-McKee-2013_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKee-2013-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> HFO is predominantly used as a fuel source for <a href="/wiki/Marine_propulsion" title="Marine propulsion">marine vessel propulsion</a> using <a href="/wiki/Marine_propulsion#Diesel" title="Marine propulsion">marine diesel engines</a> due to its relatively low cost compared to cleaner fuel sources such as <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_refining_processes#Refining_end-products" title="Petroleum refining processes">distillates</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bengtsson-2011_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bengtsson-2011-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DeCola-2018_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DeCola-2018-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The use and carriage of HFO on-board vessels presents several environmental concerns, namely the risk of <a href="/wiki/Oil_spill" title="Oil spill">oil spill</a> and the emission of toxic compounds and <a href="/wiki/Particulates" title="Particulates">particulates</a> including <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">black carbon</a>. The use of HFOs is banned as a fuel source for ships travelling in the Antarctic as part of the <a href="/wiki/International_Maritime_Organization" title="International Maritime Organization">International Maritime Organization</a>'s (IMO) <a href="/wiki/International_Code_for_Ships_Operating_in_Polar_Waters" title="International Code for Ships Operating in Polar Waters">International Code for Ships Operating in Polar Waters</a> (Polar Code).<sup id="cite_ref-IMO-2019b_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMO-2019b-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> For similar reasons, an HFO ban in Arctic waters is currently being considered.<sup id="cite_ref-MEPC_72-2018_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MEPC_72-2018-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Heavy_fuel_oil_characteristics">Heavy fuel oil characteristics</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heavy_fuel_oil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Heavy fuel oil characteristics"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>HFO consists of the remnants or residual of petroleum sources once the <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> of higher quality are extracted via processes such as thermal and <a href="/wiki/Fluid_catalytic_cracking" title="Fluid catalytic cracking">catalytic cracking</a>. Thus, HFO is also commonly referred to as residual fuel oil. The chemical composition of HFO is highly variable due to the fact that HFO is often mixed or blended with cleaner fuels; blending streams can include carbon numbers from C<sub>20</sub> to greater than C<sub>50</sub>. HFOs are blended to achieve certain viscosity and flow characteristics for a given use. As a result of the wide compositional spectrum, HFO is defined by processing, physical and final use characteristics. Being the final remnant of the cracking process, HFO also contains mixtures of the following compounds to various degrees: "paraffins, cycloparaffins, aromatics, olefins, and asphaltenes as well as molecules containing sulfur, oxygen, nitrogen and/or organometals".<sup id="cite_ref-McKee-2013_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKee-2013-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> HFO is characterized by a maximum density of 1010 kg/m<sup>3</sup> at 15°C, and a maximum viscosity of 700 mm<sup>2</sup>/s (cSt) at 50°C according to ISO 8217.<sup id="cite_ref-powerplants-2019_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-powerplants-2019-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Combustion_and_atmospheric_reactions">Combustion and atmospheric reactions</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heavy_fuel_oil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Combustion and atmospheric reactions"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Given HFO's elevated sulfur contamination (maximum of 5% by mass),<sup id="cite_ref-powerplants-2019_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-powerplants-2019-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> the combustion reaction results in the formation of sulfur dioxide SO<sub>2</sub>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Heavy_fuel_oil_use_and_shipping">Heavy fuel oil use and shipping</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heavy_fuel_oil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Heavy fuel oil use and shipping"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Since the middle of the 20th century,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> HFO has been used primarily by the <a href="/wiki/Maritime_transport" title="Maritime transport">shipping industry</a> due to its low cost compared with all other fuel oils, being up to 30% less expensive, as well as the historically lax regulatory requirements for emissions of <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">nitrogen oxides (NO<sub>x</sub>)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide (SO<sub>2</sub>)</a> by the IMO.<sup id="cite_ref-Bengtsson-2011_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bengtsson-2011-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DeCola-2018_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DeCola-2018-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> For these two reasons, HFO is the single most widely used engine fuel oil on-board ships. Data available until 2007 for global consumption of HFO at the international marine sector reports total fuel oil usages of 200 million tonnes, with HFO consumption accounting for 174 million tonnes. Data available until 2011 for fuel oil sales to the international marine shipping sector reports 207.5 million tonnes total fuel oil sales with HFO accounting for 177.9 million tonnes.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Marine vessels can use a variety of different fuels for the purpose of propulsion, which are divided into two broad categories: residual oils or distillates. In contrast to HFOs, distillates are the petroleum products created through refining crude oil and include diesel, kerosene, naphtha and gas. Residual oils are often combined to various degrees with distillates to achieve desired properties for operational and/or environmental performance. <div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Arctic_environmental_concerns">Arctic environmental concerns</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heavy_fuel_oil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Arctic environmental concerns"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Oiled_Black_Bird.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Oiled_Black_Bird.jpg/352px-Oiled_Black_Bird.jpg" decoding="async" width="352" height="264" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Oiled_Black_Bird.jpg/528px-Oiled_Black_Bird.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Oiled_Black_Bird.jpg/704px-Oiled_Black_Bird.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a><figcaption>Wildlife suffering from a tanker oil spill. Tar-like HFO coats and persistently sticks to feathers.</figcaption></figure>
<p>The use and carriage of HFO in the Arctic is a commonplace marine industry practice. In 2015, over 200 ships entered Arctic waters carrying a total of 1.1 million tonnes of fuel with 57% of fuel consumed during Arctic voyages being HFO.<sup id="cite_ref-Prior-2018_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prior-2018-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the same year, trends in carriage of HFO  were reported to be 830,000 tonnes, representing a significant growth from the reported 400,000 tonnes  in 2012. A report in 2017 by Norwegian Type Approval body <a href="/wiki/DNV_GL" class="mw-redirect" title="DNV GL">Det Norske Veritas (DNV GL)</a> calculated the total fuel use of HFO by mass in the Arctic to be over 75% with larger vessels being the main consumers. In light of increased area traffic and given that the Arctic is considered to be a sensitive ecological area with a higher response intensity to climate change, the environmental risks posed by HFO present concern for  environmentalists and governments in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The two main environmental concerns for HFO in the Arctic are the risk of spill or accidental discharge and the emission of black carbon as a result of HFO consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-Prior-2018_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prior-2018-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DeCola-2018_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DeCola-2018-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Environmental_impacts_of_heavy_fuel_oil_spills">Environmental impacts of heavy fuel oil spills</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heavy_fuel_oil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts of heavy fuel oil spills"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Due to its very high viscosity and elevated density, HFO released into the environment is a greater threat to flora and fauna compared to distillate or other residual fuels. In 2009, the Arctic Council identified the spill of oil in the Arctic as the greatest threat to the local marine environment. Being the remnant of the distillation and cracking processes, HFO is characterized by an elevated overall toxicity compared to all other fuels. Its viscosity prevents breakdown into the environment, a property exacerbated by the cold temperatures in the Arctic resulting in the formation of tar-lumps, and an increase in volume through emulsification. Its density, tendency to persist and emulsify can result in HFO polluting both the water column and seabed.<sup id="cite_ref-Prior-2018_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prior-2018-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="History_of_major_HFO_spills_from_2000_onward">History of major HFO spills from 2000 onward</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heavy_fuel_oil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History of major HFO spills from 2000 onward"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The following HFO specific spills have occurred since the year 2000. The information is organized according to year and ship name and includes amount released and the spill location:
</p>
<ul><li>2000 Janra, Germany (40 tons in the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_%C3%85land" title="Sea of Åland">Sea of Åland</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>

<li>2002 <a href="/wiki/Prestige_oil_spill" title="Prestige oil spill"><i>Prestige</i> oil spill</a>, Spain (17.8 million gallons in <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>)</li>
<li>2003 Fu Shan Hai, China (1,680 tons in the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>)</li>
<li>2004 Selendang Ayu, Malaysia (336,000 gallons in Unalaska Island - near Arctic)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Full_City_oil_spill" title="Full City oil spill">2009 Full City</a>, Panama (6,300-9,500 gallons in <a href="/wiki/Langesund" title="Langesund">Langesund</a>)</li>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Environmental_impacts_of_heavy_fuel_oil_use">Environmental impacts of heavy fuel oil use</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heavy_fuel_oil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts of heavy fuel oil use"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The combustion of HFO in ship engines results in the highest amount of <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">black carbon</a> emissions compared to all other fuels. The choice of marine fuel is the most important determinant of ship engine emission factors for black carbon. The second most important factor in the emission of black carbon is the ship load size, with emission factors of black carbon increasing up to six times given low engine loads.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Black carbon is the product of incomplete combustion and a component of soot and fine particulate matter (&lt;2.5 μg). It has a short atmospheric lifetime of a few days to a week and is typically removed upon precipitation events.<sup id="cite_ref-Bellouin-2015_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bellouin-2015-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Although there has been debate concerning the <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a>  of black carbon, combinations of ground and satellite observations suggest a global solar absorption of 0.9W·m<sup>−2</sup>, making it the second most important climate forcer after CO<sub>2.</sub><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ramanathan-2008_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ramanathan-2008-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Black carbon affects the climate system by: decreasing the snow/ice albedo through dark soot deposits and increasing snowmelt timing,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> reducing the planetary albedo through absorption of solar radiation reflected by the cloud systems, earth surface and atmosphere,<sup id="cite_ref-Ramanathan-2008_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ramanathan-2008-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> as well as directly decreasing <a href="/wiki/Cloud_albedo" title="Cloud albedo">cloud albedo</a> with black carbon contamination of water and ice found therein.<sup id="cite_ref-Ramanathan-2008_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ramanathan-2008-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bellouin-2015_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bellouin-2015-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The greatest increase in Arctic surface temperature per unit of black carbon emissions results from the decrease in snow/ice albedo which makes Arctic specific black carbon release more detrimental than emissions elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="IMO_and_the_Polar_Code">IMO and the Polar Code</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heavy_fuel_oil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: IMO and the Polar Code"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The International Maritime Organization (IMO), a specialized arm of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, adopted into force on 1 January 2017 the <a href="/wiki/International_Code_for_Ships_Operating_in_Polar_Waters" title="International Code for Ships Operating in Polar Waters">International Code for Ships Operating in Polar Waters</a> or Polar Code. The requirements of the Polar Code are mandatory under both the <a href="/wiki/MARPOL_73/78" title="MARPOL 73/78">International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships (MARPOL)</a> and the <a href="/wiki/SOLAS_Convention" title="SOLAS Convention">International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea (SOLAS)</a>. The two broad categories covered by the Polar Code include safety and pollution prevention related to navigation in both Arctic and Antarctic polar waters.<sup id="cite_ref-IMO-2019b_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMO-2019b-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The carriage and use of HFO in the Arctic is discouraged by the Polar Code while being banned completely from the Antarctic under MARPOL Annex I regulation 43.<sup id="cite_ref-IMO-2019a_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMO-2019a-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The ban of HFO use and carriage in the Antarctic precedes the adoption of the Polar Code. At its 60th session (26 March 2010), The Marine Environmental Protection Committee (MEPC) adopted Resolution 189(60) which went into effect in 2011 and prohibits fuels of the following characteristics:<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote>
<ol><li>crude oils having a density at 15°C higher than 900 kg/m<sup>3</sup>&#160;;</li>
<li>oils, other than crude oils, having a density at 15°C higher than 900 kg/m<sup>3</sup> or a kinematic viscosity at 50°C higher than 180 mm<sup>2</sup> /s; or</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bitumen" title="Bitumen">bitumen</a>, tar and their emulsions.</li></ol>
</blockquote>
<p>IMO's Marine Environmental Protection Committee (MEPC) tasked the Pollution Prevention Response Sub-Committee (PPR) to enact a ban on the use and carriage of heavy fuel in Arctic waters at its 72nd and 73rd sessions. This task is also accompanied by a requirement to properly define HFO taking into account its current definition under  MARPOL Annex I regulation 43.<sup id="cite_ref-IMO-2019a_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMO-2019a-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The adoption of the ban is anticipated for 2021, with widespread implementation by 2023.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Resistance_to_heavy_fuel_oil_phase-out">Resistance to heavy fuel oil phase-out</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heavy_fuel_oil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Resistance to heavy fuel oil phase-out"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The Clean Arctic Alliance was the first IMO delegate nonprofit organization to campaign against the use of HFO in Arctic waters. However, the phase-out and ban of HFO in the Arctic was formally proposed to MEPC by eight countries in 2018: Finland, Germany, Iceland, the Netherlands, New Zealand, Norway, Sweden and the United States. <sup id="cite_ref-Prior-2018_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prior-2018-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-IMO-2019a_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMO-2019a-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Although these member states continue to support the initiative, several countries have been vocal about their resistance to an HFO ban on such a short time scale. The Russian Federation has expressed concern for impacts to the maritime shipping industry and trade given the relatively low cost of HFO. Russia instead suggested the development and implementation of mitigation measures for the use and carriage of HFO in Arctic waters. Canada and Marshall Islands have presented similar arguments, highlighting the potential impacts on Arctic communities (namely remote indigenous populations) and economies.<sup id="cite_ref-MEPC_72-2018_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MEPC_72-2018-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>To appease concerns and resistance, at its 6th session in February 2019, the PPR sub-committee working group developed a "draft methodology for analyzing impacts" of HFO to be finalized at PPR's 7th session in 2020. The purpose of the methodology being to evaluate the ban according to its economic and social impacts on Arctic indigenous communities and other local communities, to measure anticipated benefits to local ecosystems, and potentially consider other factors that could be positively or negatively affected by the ban.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
<br />
</p>
