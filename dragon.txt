<p>The <b>Dragon Spacecraft Qualification Unit</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Boilerplate_(spaceflight)" title="Boilerplate (spaceflight)">boilerplate</a> version of the <a href="/wiki/SpaceX_Dragon" title="SpaceX Dragon">Dragon</a> spacecraft manufactured by <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a>. After using it for ground tests to rate Dragon's shape and mass in various tests, SpaceX launched it into <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> on the maiden flight of the <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a> rocket, on June 4, 2010. SpaceX used the launch to evaluate the aerodynamic conditions on the spacecraft and performance of the carrier rocket in a real-world launch scenario, ahead of Dragon flights for <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Commercial_Orbital_Transportation_Services" title="Commercial Orbital Transportation Services">Commercial Orbital Transportation Services</a> program. The spacecraft orbited the Earth over 300 times before decaying from orbit and reentering the atmosphere on 27 June.<sup id="cite_ref-space.com_Fiery_End_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-space.com_Fiery_End-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Delays">Delays</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragon_Spacecraft_Qualification_Unit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Delays"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In September 2009, the launch was slated to occur no earlier than November 29, 2009,<sup id="cite_ref-Block_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Block-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> however the launch was subsequently postponed ten more times, to launch dates in February, March, April, May, and June 2010,<sup id="cite_ref-spaceflightnow_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spaceflightnow-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> for multiple reasons including finding an open launch date, approvals, and retesting. The launch date was eventually set for June 4, 2010.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Processing">Processing</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragon_Spacecraft_Qualification_Unit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Processing"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>On October 16, 2009, nine <a href="/wiki/Merlin_1C" class="mw-redirect" title="Merlin 1C">Merlin 1C</a> engines of the first stage of the Falcon&#160;9 rocket intended to launch the Dragon were test fired at SpaceX's <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine_test_facility" title="Rocket engine test facility">rocket engine test facility</a> in <a href="/wiki/McGregor,_Texas" title="McGregor, Texas">McGregor, Texas</a>. On January&#160;2, 2010, the second stage of the Falcon&#160;9 vehicle was test fired for the full duration required for orbital insertion, 345&#160;seconds. By late February, the launch vehicle had been assembled and raised to its vertical position on the launch pad at <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station_Space_Launch_Complex_40" class="mw-redirect" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station Space Launch Complex 40">Cape Canaveral Air Force Station Space Launch Complex 40</a> (SLC-40),<sup id="cite_ref-Atkinson_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atkinson-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> having been rolled out to the launch pad on February&#160;19.<sup id="cite_ref-SpaceX_February_19,_2010_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SpaceX_February_19,_2010-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>On March 13, 2010, the first stage engines successfully underwent a 3.5 second static test firing,<sup id="cite_ref-space.com_March_15,_2010_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-space.com_March_15,_2010-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> having failed a previous attempt the day before.<sup id="cite_ref-Halvorson_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Halvorson-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Launch">Launch</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragon_Spacecraft_Qualification_Unit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Launch"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>SpaceX announced in September 2009 that the Dragon Spacecraft Qualification Unit would be the payload for the first Falcon 9 launch.<sup id="cite_ref-Update-0909_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Update-0909-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> At the time, launch was scheduled to occur no earlier than November 2009. The launch date had been delayed several times for various reasons, The spacecraft was launched and entered orbit on June 4, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-SpaceX_7_June_2010_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SpaceX_7_June_2010-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The first actual launch attempt targeted a four-hour launch window opening at 15:00 UTC (11 a.m. <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Time_Zone" title="Eastern Time Zone">EDT</a>) on 4 June 2010,<sup id="cite_ref-foxnews_launch_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-foxnews_launch-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> with the possibility of a launch attempt the following day in the event that launch did not occur inside the 4 June window. The first attempt to launch the rocket, at 17:30 UTC, was aborted seconds prior to liftoff due to a reported out of range engine parameter, which later turned out to be a sensor error. The launch was rescheduled, with a successful liftoff taking place an hour and fifteen minutes later at 18:45 UTC (2:45&#160;pm EDT).<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_launch_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_launch-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The vehicle reached orbit successfully, entering into a 250&#160;km (160&#160;mi) orbit.<sup id="cite_ref-CNN_launch_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNN_launch-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<p>SpaceX employee Ken Bowersox stated that the rocket experienced "a little bit of roll at liftoff". This roll had stopped prior to the craft reaching the top of the lightning towers.<sup id="cite_ref-Bowersox_interview_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bowersox_interview-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A separate issue involved a moderate, uncorrected roll at the end of the second stage firing.<sup id="cite_ref-msnbc20100604_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc20100604-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The first stage, that is designed to be reusable, disintegrated during reentry, before the parachutes could be deployed.<sup id="cite_ref-parachute_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parachute-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Orbit">Orbit</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragon_Spacecraft_Qualification_Unit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Orbit"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Following the launch, SpaceX left the qualification unit in low Earth orbit, where its orbit was allowed to decay and it reentered the atmosphere around 00:50 GMT on June 27, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-spaceflightnow.com_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spaceflightnow.com-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The qualification unit remained attached to the second stage of the launcher; production units separate for orbital maneuvering.<sup id="cite_ref-updates_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-updates-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>SpaceX lost contact with the Dragon and the Falcon 9 second stage shortly after orbit was achieved, as the on-board batteries were only designed to last long enough to launch. They re-entered in the early morning hours (UTC) on June 27, 2010. Although exact location is uncertain, it is believed to have disintegrated over <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-spaceflightnow.com_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spaceflightnow.com-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<p>At around 5:30&#160;am local time on June 5, 2010, sightings of a mysterious "lollipop-type swirl" light or cloud heading from west to east were reported in the Australian states of <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Capital_Territory" title="Australian Capital Territory">Australian Capital Territory</a>. The sightings were likened to the Russian <a href="/wiki/RSM-56_Bulava" title="RSM-56 Bulava">RSM-56 Bulava</a> rocket launch that prompted similar video and images from the Arctic known as the <a href="/wiki/2009_Norwegian_spiral_anomaly" title="2009 Norwegian spiral anomaly">2009 Norwegian spiral anomaly</a>; it was suggested that the visible object was the spent upper stage or the qualification unit launched aboard the Falcon 9 or both.<sup id="cite_ref-mates_see_UFO_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mates_see_UFO-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
